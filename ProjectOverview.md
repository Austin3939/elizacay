## What This Site Is

Your site at does three things:

1. **Showcases your work** — the gallery and shop give people a place to browse and buy

## The Strategy: The Flywheel

Everything on this site is designed around one idea — **the flywheel**. This is the core focus of the site and the reason certain features exist. Understanding it will help you get the most out of every page.

```
TikTok / Instagram / Pinterest
        ↓  discovery — drives cold traffic in
      Your Site
        ↓  conversion — turns visitors into subscribers
    Your Email List
        ↓  retention — announces prints, commission slots, process updates
      Sales + Commissions
        ↓  happy buyers share your work
        ↓  feeds back into social discovery
```

Each platform has a specific job:

- **TikTok** — your fastest path to new eyes. Process videos, speedpaints, and timelapse reach people who have never heard of you. The algorithm actively pushes your content to cold audiences if you post consistently. This is your top-of-funnel engine.

- **Instagram** — your portfolio layer. People who discover you on TikTok come here to browse your aesthetic and decide if they like your work. Link your site in bio. Your grid should look like your brand.

- **Pinterest** — long-tail discovery. Botanical and illustrative art does exceptionally well on Pinterest. A single well-titled pin can drive traffic to your site for 18 months or longer. Pin every journal post and every product.

- **Your Email List** — the only audience you truly own. Followers disappear if a platform changes its algorithm or shuts down. Your email list is yours forever. Every page on this site is designed to nudge people toward subscribing.

### The Content Engine: One Piece, Four Places

The **Process Journal** (see below) is your content home base. The rhythm that makes this sustainable:

**Weekly:**
- Write one short Journal post on your site (a WIP photo, a sketch, a thought about a piece you're working on — doesn't need to be long)
- Adapt it for Instagram (post the image with a caption drawn from the journal entry)
- Pin it to Pinterest with a descriptive title (e.g. "botanical ink illustration in progress — bell flower study")
- Use it as context or B-roll for a TikTok video

**Monthly:**
- Send one newsletter email to your list — a roundup of what you've been making, any new prints added to the shop, a behind-the-scenes moment, and a reminder that commission slots are limited and subscribers hear first

**Quarterly:**
- Open commission slots — email list hears 48 hours before you announce on social
- This trains your subscribers to stay subscribed, because they get real early access

### Why This Works

You're not chasing the algorithm. You're using social to fill the top of a funnel that you control. Someone finds a Instagram post, comes to your site, signs up for the list. Now you can reach them directly, forever, regardless of what any platform does next.

**The flywheel is the most important feature on this site.** Every decision about what to post, what to build next, and how to spend your time should come back to this question: does it drive discovery, convert visitors, or retain subscribers?

### Shop

The shop sells your prints and apparel, organised into two tabs. It connects directly to your Shopify store and updates automatically as you add products.

#### How to Use This Page

- **Limited editions matter.** If prints are truly limited (e.g. edition of 50), say so on the product. Scarcity is honest and it drives urgency. "12 of 50 remaining" is more compelling than "buy now."

- When you launch a new print, announce it to your email list first (48 hours before social). This rewards subscribers and trains people to stay on your list.
- Share individual product pages on Instagram stories and link them directly in your Pinterest pins — not just the homepage.

### Process Journal

*This page is being added to the site.* It's a simple feed of short posts — a sketch, a WIP photo, a thought about something you're making. It doesn't need to be long or polished. It's your studio diary, made public.

This is the most important new feature on the site and the engine that makes the flywheel work.

#### How to Use This Page

- Post once a week. It can be as simple as one photo and two sentences: *"Working on a new bell flower piece today. Playing with negative space more than usual — something about the emptiness feels right for this one."*
- Every journal post becomes content for all three platforms:
  - **Instagram:** post the image with the journal text as your caption, link to the full post in bio
  - **Pinterest:** pin the image with a descriptive title that includes keywords (e.g. "botanical ink illustration in progress — bell flower study")
  - **TikTok:** film yourself working on the piece, reference the journal post, link in bio
- Pull two or three journal moments into your monthly newsletter — this is most of your email content already written
- Over time, journal posts build SEO for searches like "botanical illustration process" and "commission illustrator" — which brings in organic traffic from Google and Pinterest without any paid advertising

#### How to Use This Page for Audience Growth

The journal is the engine of the flywheel. Without regular content, the social platforms go quiet, the email list goes cold, and the site becomes a static brochure. Consistency here matters more than quality — a real, slightly imperfect post every week beats a perfectly polished post once a month.

If you ever don't know what to post, use this template:
- *What I'm working on right now*
- *What's interesting or challenging about it*
- *Where it's headed*

That's a complete journal post. Three sentences is enough.

## Email List & Newsletter

Your email list is the most important thing you'll build alongside your art. It's the only audience you own outright — no algorithm, no platform risk.

### Setting Up

You'll need an email marketing platform. Recommended options:

- **Klaviyo** — best if you want deep integration with Shopify (can trigger automatic emails based on purchases, abandoned carts, etc.). Free up to 250 subscribers.
- **Mailchimp** — simpler and very well-known. Free up to 500 subscribers. Good starting point.
- **Kit (formerly ConvertKit)** — built for creators, excellent for managing subscribers with different interests. Free up to 1,000 subscribers.

Once you've chosen a platform, your developer can connect the subscribe form on the site so every sign-up flows in automatically.

> **Open question** Which email platform would you like to use? If you're unsure, start with Mailchimp — easiest to get going, free until you grow.

### What to Send

**Monthly newsletter — suggested structure:**
1. A short personal note (2–3 sentences — what you've been working on, what's inspiring you lately)
2. Two or three highlights from your Process Journal that month
3. What's new in the shop (new prints, restocks, anything limited)
4. Commission update (slots open / waitlist / next opening date)
5. A reminder that subscribers hear about new work first

Keep it short. People open emails from artists they like — don't make it feel like homework to read.

### The Subscriber Value Proposition

This exact phrasing should appear consistently across the site wherever the subscribe form appears:

> *"Join the list for first access to new prints and commission slots, plus process updates from the studio."*

This works because it's specific. "First access" means something real — a subscriber who misses a sold-out print will remember they should have been on the list.

### What to Post Where

| Platform | Content type | Frequency | Goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| TikTok | Process videos, speedpaints, timelapse, "how I made this" | 3–5x per week | Discovery — reach cold audiences who don't know you yet |
| Instagram | Finished work, WIP photos, studio moments, process close-ups | 4–5x per week | Portfolio — convert browsers into followers and buyers |
| Pinterest | Every product, every journal post, every finished piece | Daily if possible | Long-tail traffic — evergreen discovery that keeps working |

### TikTok Tips for Artists Starting Out

- **Show the process, not just the result.** Videos of hands drawing, ink drying, the sketch evolving perform far better than a reveal of the finished piece.
- **Talk to camera occasionally.** Even once a month. People follow people, not just art.
- **Use on-screen text.** Many viewers watch on mute. Text like *"drawing botanicals with a 0.05mm pen"* tells people exactly what they're watching without needing sound.
- **Don't wait until it's perfect.** Post consistently from day one. Early videos don't need to be good — they teach you what resonates.

### Pinterest Tips for Illustrators

- **Titles matter more than captions.** Write titles like: *"Botanical ink illustration — Bell Flower Study print"* not just *"Bell Flower."*
- **Link every pin back to your site** — to the journal post, the product page, or the gallery. Pinterest is a traffic machine if you link correctly and a dead end if you don't.
- **Create boards for your subjects** — "Botanical Illustration", "Floral Art Prints", "Drawing Process" — and pin your work into them consistently from day one.
